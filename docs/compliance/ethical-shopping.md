# Ethical Shopping Guidelines

> **Not legal advice.** This is a practical risk framework and set of product guardrails, written
> for counsel to review before launch.

## Why this document exists (internal)

PennyForge's entire competitive position rests on trust: receipt-verified reports, a community
that polices its own accuracy, and a compliance posture retailers can live with (see `CLAUDE.md`
and `docs/compliance.md`). Retailers broadly tolerate respectful deal-hunting — clearance and
penny-out markdowns are their own inventory processes, and a polite shopper buying a marked-down
item is a normal transaction. What they do not tolerate, and what generates viral backlash, store
policy crackdowns, and legal exposure, is confrontational or fraudulent behavior filmed for clout.

One bad actor going viral — berating a cashier, swapping tags, ransacking a stockroom — damages
every member of the community and hands retailers a reason to treat the whole hobby as hostile.
Ethics is therefore not a PR page; it is the product's moat. These guidelines are enforced through
moderation (see `docs/compliance/ugc-policy.md`) and are written so the community-facing sections
below can be lifted nearly verbatim into an in-app "Community Guidelines / Shop Ethically" page.

Sections marked **[community-facing copy]** are written in second person for direct in-app use.
Sections marked **[internal]** are notes for the team and counsel.

## The golden rules **[community-facing copy]**

PennyForge works because store employees, other shoppers, and retailers can coexist with us. These
rules are how we keep it that way. They're not fine print — they're the deal.

### Be kind to store employees

Employees don't set prices, don't control the markdown system, and don't owe you a penny sale.
They're often the lowest-paid people in the building dealing with the fallout of decisions made
far above them. A friendly, patient shopper is remembered; so is a rude one.

- **If the register price differs from what you expected, or the store declines the sale, accept
  it politely and walk away.** Never argue, never demand, never escalate to a manager to pressure
  the outcome, and never come back later to try a different cashier. A declined sale is the
  store's call to make.
- **Don't call stores** to ask employees to run stock checks or confirm prices for you. That's
  someone's workday you're consuming for a maybe.
- **Don't fish for internal information.** Markdown schedules, backroom counts, "when does the
  system update" — if an employee has to check an internal system or bend a rule to answer you,
  don't ask. Publicly posted signage and what's on the shelf are fair; everything else isn't yours
  to extract.

### The shelf rule

If an item is physically out on the sales floor where any customer could pick it up, it's fair
game to buy. That's the whole rule — and it cuts both ways:

- **No destructive digging.** Don't tear apart displays, dump bins, or leave a shelf looking like
  a storm hit it.
- **Don't open sealed boxes or packaging** to check contents or find a scannable barcode.
- **Never climb racking or shelving**, and never enter back rooms, stockrooms, receiving areas, or
  any employee-only space. If it's not on the floor, it's not available.
- **Leave displays tidy — better than you found them.** Thirty seconds of re-facing a shelf is the
  cheapest goodwill you'll ever buy.

### Honesty at the register

- **Never alter, swap, remove, or fabricate tags, stickers, or barcodes.** Putting a clearance tag
  on a different item, printing your own labels, or scanning a barcode that isn't the item's own
  is fraud, full stop. It's illegal, it's bannable, and we will report it.
- **Return fraud** — buying at a penny and returning at full price, receipt games, box-swapping —
  is theft. Same answer: prohibited, bannable, reportable.

### Share the floor

- **Don't hide items around the store** to "save them for later." Stashing merchandise behind
  other products or in another aisle deprives other shoppers and creates work for employees. Buy
  it now or leave it for the next person.
- **Don't clear shelves of essentials.** Be especially considerate with baby formula, infant and
  medical supplies, medicine, and food staples. A great price on formula is someone else's ability
  to feed their kid — take what your household needs and leave the rest.
- **Respect posted store policies and purchase limits.** Limits exist so more people get a shot.
  Splitting transactions or making repeat trips to dodge a limit is against the spirit of
  everything here.
- **Basic cart courtesy.** Don't block aisles staging a haul, don't leave a loaded cart parked
  across an endcap while you scout, and keep kids and carts out of other shoppers' way. You're
  sharing the store, not occupying it.

## Penny items, honestly explained **[community-facing copy]**

Let's be straight about what a penny item actually is, because the mythology helps no one.

At some retailers, when a clearance item reaches the end of its markdown cycle, the system drops
its price to $0.01 as an internal signal that the item should be pulled from the floor and
disposed of or returned to the vendor. That's it. It's an inventory-removal flag, not a secret
code, not a prize, and not something anyone is entitled to.

What that means in practice:

- **If a penny item is on the shelf and rings up at a penny, buying it is legal and completely
  fine.** The store's system offered a price; you paid it. Enjoy the find and post the receipt.
- **The store may legitimately refuse the sale and pull the item.** Many chains instruct
  employees to do exactly that, because the item was supposed to be off the floor already. That's
  their right and their inventory. Say "no problem," hand it over, and move on.
- **What's never fine:** pressuring or guilt-tripping staff into completing the sale, digging
  through backstock or employee areas hunting for pulled items, or misrepresenting tags or
  barcodes to force a penny price. The line between "lucky find" and "problem customer" is exactly
  the line between buying what's on the shelf and manufacturing the outcome.

PennyForge exists to share real, first-hand finds — an item you saw, a price it actually rang at,
a receipt that proves it. That's the whole game, and it only stays fun if we play it clean.

## Reselling ethics **[community-facing copy]**

Plenty of our best contributors are resellers, and that's welcome here. Buying clearance low and
selling it for what it's worth is legal, normal commerce — the route planner's profit math exists
for you. The ethics above still apply, plus a few that come with the territory:

- **No shelf-clearing essentials for resale.** The formula/medicine/food-staples rule applies
  double when the motive is margin.
- **Never misrepresent condition or authenticity.** "New" means new and sealed. Damaged, opened,
  or shelf-worn items get described as exactly that.
- **Follow the rules of wherever you sell.** Marketplace policies (eBay, Amazon, Facebook
  Marketplace, etc.) on restricted categories, condition claims, and pricing are your
  responsibility.
- **Never resell recalled goods.** Selling a recalled product is a federal violation under the
  Consumer Product Safety Act, even if you bought it legitimately at clearance. Check the CPSC
  recall list before listing anything in categories like children's products, small appliances,
  or furniture. When in doubt, don't list it.

## How we enforce this **[community-facing copy]**

These guidelines have teeth. Posts that celebrate, instruct, or joke-but-not-really about
confronting employees, tag or barcode tricks, hiding items, shelf-clearing essentials, or any
form of return fraud get removed — no matter how good the find was. Repeat offenders are banned.
The full moderation policy, including how reports are reviewed and what earns a strike versus an
immediate ban, lives in our Community Content Policy.

If you see behavior here that crosses these lines — in a post, a photo, or a comment — report it
through moderation. Flagging bad behavior isn't snitching; it's how a trust-based community stays
worth being part of. The people who keep this hobby sustainable are the ones who hold the line.
